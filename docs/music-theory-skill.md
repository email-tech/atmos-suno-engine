# Musical Foundations (Music Theory & Ear Training) Skill Specification

## Overview

This skill covers the foundational musical knowledge the app relies on: notation, pitch, scales, intervals, chords, keys, harmony, rhythm, meter, and basic ear training. It defines how the app explains music, analyzes examples, and generates exercises for theory and listening.

## Core Subskills

- Pitch and notation: note names, clefs, accidentals, staff notation, enharmonic equivalents.
- Scales and modes: major, minor, common modes, scale degrees, and functional roles.
- Intervals: quality and size of intervals, consonance/dissonance, melodic vs harmonic intervals.
- Chords and harmony: triads, seventh chords, chord qualities, diatonic harmony, tonic-dominant relationships.
- Keys and tonality: key signatures, relative and parallel keys, tonal centers, modulation basics.
- Rhythm and meter: pulse, beat hierarchy, simple/compound meters, syncopation.
- Ear training: recognizing intervals, chords, progressions, rhythms, and basic melodic patterns.

## Key Concepts

- Music is organized in terms of pitch (frequency) and time (rhythm). Notation provides a symbolic system for representing both.
- Scales define the pitch collection of a key; chords are built by stacking scale degrees, typically in thirds, to create harmonic functions.
- Functional harmony describes how chords relate to a tonal center (tonic, predominant, dominant) and create tension and resolution.
- Rhythm and meter organize time into recurring patterns of strong and weak beats; syncopation and subdivision add complexity.
- Ear training reinforces theory by helping learners connect sounds to theoretical labels (interval names, chord types, progressions, rhythmic patterns).

## Tools, Techniques, and Workflows

- Theory explanation workflow: introduce concept (e.g. scale), show notation, demonstrate audio, then provide interactive exercises.
- Analysis workflow: identify key, scale, primary chords, cadences, and voice-leading; label functions and discuss tension and release.
- Ear training workflow: start with isolated intervals and chords, then move to short melodies and progressions in a limited set of keys, gradually increasing complexity.
- Visualization tools: staff notation, piano keyboard diagrams, fretboard diagrams, and rhythm grids.

## Learning Progression

### Beginner

- Read and write notes in treble and bass clefs.
- Understand whole and half steps, major and minor scales, and simple major/minor triads.
- Recognize and tap simple rhythms in 4/4 and 3/4.
- Identify a few basic intervals and chord qualities by ear.

### Intermediate

- Work with modes, key changes, diatonic seventh chords, and common progressions (e.g. II-V-I, I-IV-V).
- Analyze short pieces or songs: key, functional harmony, cadences, phrase structure.
- Hear and label more complex intervals, seventh chords, and common progressions.
- Understand simple modulation and borrowed chords.

### Advanced

- Use extended harmony (9ths, 11ths, 13ths), modal mixture, and secondary dominants fluently.
- Analyze full forms (binary, ternary, verse–chorus, sonata-like structures) with attention to harmonic rhythm and large-scale tension.
- Recognize complex rhythms, asymmetric meters, and polyrhythms by ear.
- Connect theoretical concepts across styles (classical, jazz, pop, film).

## Common Mistakes and Misconceptions

- Treating theory as rules rather than descriptive tools; the app should frame theory as patterns, not rigid laws.
- Confusing key signatures with pitch collections (e.g. ignoring accidentals and modal behavior).
- Overloading beginners with advanced harmony before fundamentals are solid.
- Neglecting ear training, leading to purely symbolic understanding without sound.
